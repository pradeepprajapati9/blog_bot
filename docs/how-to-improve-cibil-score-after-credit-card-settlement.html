How to Improve CIBIL Score After Credit Card Settlement

27 Jun 2026

A credit card settlement might feel like a major relief when you are struggling with heavy debt, but it leaves a lasting scar on your credit profile. When you settle, the bank accepts a lower amount than what you actually owe and reports the account as Settled to CIBIL, rather than Closed. This negative status drops your credit score significantly and makes future lenders hesitant to approve your loan applications. However, a low score is not permanent. With patience and a strategic approach, you can rebuild your creditworthiness. Here is a practical, step-by-step guide to help you recover and improve your CIBIL score after a credit card settlement.

Why a Credit Card Settlement Lowers Your CIBIL Score

When you opt for a credit card settlement, the lender writes off a portion of your outstanding debt. While this stops recovery calls and provides temporary peace of mind, CIBIL categorizes this account as Settled. This status remains in your credit history for up to seven years. Future lenders view this as a sign that you did not repay your full financial obligation, marking you as a high-risk borrower. This status can instantly reduce your CIBIL score by 50 to 100 points. Understanding this is crucial because it helps you realize that repairing your score is a gradual process. Before you can improve your score, you must stop applying for traditional unsecured credit cards or personal loans, as these inquiries will lead to immediate rejections and pull your score down further.

Get a Secured Credit Card to Rebuild Credit History

One of the fastest and safest ways to rebuild your credit after a settlement is by getting a secured credit card. Since traditional lenders might reject your application for a regular credit card, a secured card is an excellent alternative. Most major Indian banks offer secured credit cards against a fixed deposit. You typically get a credit limit worth 80 percent to 90 percent of your deposit amount. The bank faces zero risk because your fixed deposit acts as collateral. By using this card for small, regular purchases and paying the bill in full every month, you start generating fresh, positive credit history. Over time, these consistent payments report to CIBIL, gradually diluting the negative impact of your past settlement and lifting your credit score back up.

Prioritize 100 Percent Timely Bill Payments

Your payment history is the single most important factor determining your CIBIL score, accounting for about 35 percent of the total calculation. After a settlement, you cannot afford even a single late payment. Whether you are paying your newly acquired secured credit card bills, utility bills, or any existing active loans, make sure you pay them on time, every single month. Set up auto-debit facilities with your bank or configure calendar alerts so you never miss a due date. Even a one-day delay can be reported to credit bureaus and damage your recovery efforts. Consistent, timely payments over a period of 12 to 24 months demonstrate to credit bureaus and future lenders that you have developed disciplined financial habits and are now a responsible borrower.

Maintain a Low Credit Utilization Ratio

Credit Utilization Ratio is the percentage of your available credit limit that you actually use. For example, if your secured credit card has a limit of 50,000 Rupees and you spend 40,000 Rupees, your credit utilization is 80 percent. Lenders and credit bureaus view a high utilization ratio as a sign of credit hunger and financial stress. To improve your CIBIL score, aim to keep your credit utilization below 30 percent of your total limit across all active cards. In our example, this means spending no more than 15,000 Rupees on that card. If you need to make a larger purchase, try paying off a portion of your balance before the official credit card statement is generated. This ensures that the reported utilization remains low, boosting your score.

Try to Convert the Settled Status to Closed

If you find yourself in a better financial position in the future, you can approach your original lender to convert your Settled status to Closed. To do this, contact the bank that issued the settled card and request to pay the remaining unpaid balance, which is the difference between your settlement amount and the original outstanding amount. Once you pay this remaining dues balance plus any agreed-upon interest, the bank will issue a No Dues Certificate. The bank will then report this updated status to CIBIL, changing your account status from Settled to Closed. While this process requires financial outflow, a Closed status looks significantly better to future lenders and accelerates your credit score recovery.

Monitor Your CIBIL Report for Errors

Rebuilding your credit requires close monitoring of your credit report. You are entitled to one free credit report every year from each of the major credit bureaus in India, including CIBIL. Check your report regularly to ensure that your secured card payments are being reported accurately and that your settled account status reflects correctly. Occasionally, banks make clerical errors, such as failing to update your payment status or listing a settled account as active or overdue. If you spot any discrepancies, raise a formal dispute on the official CIBIL website immediately. The bureau will verify the details with the respective bank and correct any errors within 30 days. Ensuring your report is completely error-free is vital to maximizing your score improvement efforts.

Conclusion

Improving your CIBIL score after a credit card settlement requires time, patience, and consistent financial discipline. While the Settled status remains on your report, its negative impact naturally weakens over time as you build fresh, positive credit history. By securing a fixed deposit-backed card, paying all bills on time, keeping your credit utilization low, and eventually working to fully close the old debt, you can steadily climb back to a healthy credit score of 750 or above.
